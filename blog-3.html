How to Brief a Web Designer (And Get Better Results)

6 min read  ·  By ScriptaForge

One of the most common reasons web design projects go over budget, over deadline, or end in disappointment isn't bad design — it's a bad brief. The better the information you give your designer upfront, the less time is wasted on revisions, and the more likely you are to end up with a site you love. Here's exactly what to include.

1. Explain your business clearly

Don't assume your designer knows what your business does. Write a short paragraph covering: what you sell or offer, who your customers are, and what makes you different from competitors. This context shapes every design decision — from the tone of the copy to the colours chosen. The more specific you are, the better.

Example: "We're a mobile dog grooming service in Manchester. Our customers are busy professionals and families with medium to large dogs. We come to them — no drop-off needed. Our main differentiator is that we use all-natural, hypoallergenic products."

2. Define what success looks like

What do you want the website to actually do? Common goals include:

  • Generate leads and enquiries through a contact form
  • Drive phone calls from local customers
  • Sell products online via an e-commerce store
  • Build credibility so customers trust you before they meet you
  • Allow clients to book appointments directly

Pick your top one or two goals and be specific. "Look professional" is not a goal. "Get 10 enquiry form submissions per month" is a goal.

3. Describe your target customer

Who is your ideal client? Age range, income level, location, what they care about, what problems they're trying to solve. The more you know about your customer, the more your website can speak directly to them. A website built for a 55-year-old retiree looks and reads very differently to one built for a 25-year-old entrepreneur.

4. Share examples of sites you like (and why)

Find three to five websites — from any industry — where you think "I'd love something like this." Send the links to your designer with a note on what specifically you like about each one. Is it the colour palette? The photography? The layout? The tone of the copy? "I like this site" tells us nothing. "I like how clean and minimal this layout is, with the big bold headlines" tells us everything.

5. Be honest about your budget

Many clients are reluctant to share their budget, worried it will anchor the price high. But knowing your budget actually helps a designer scope the project correctly and tell you honestly what's achievable. There's no point designing a 20-page custom e-commerce site for someone with a £500 budget — that wastes everyone's time. Share your real number so you get a realistic proposal.

6. Tell us what content you already have

Do you have a logo? Brand colours? Existing photography? Copy already written for any pages? The answers affect the timeline and cost significantly. If you need everything created from scratch — logo, photos, copy, and design — that's a bigger project than if you're just redesigning around existing assets. Be upfront about what you have and what you need.

7. Share your timeline

Do you have a hard launch date? A product launch, a trade show, or a marketing campaign that the website needs to be live for? Tell your designer upfront. Most agencies and freelancers can work to tight deadlines — but only if they know about them from the start. Springing a two-week deadline on someone at the halfway point never ends well.
